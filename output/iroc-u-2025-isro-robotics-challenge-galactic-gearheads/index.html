From Campus to Cosmos: IRoC-U 2025 And The Rise of Galactic Gearheads

IRoC-U 2025: How ISRO’s Robotics Challenge...

 Is Preparing India’s Youth for Mars Exploration




India’s space ambitions are entering a bold new phase. From lunar landings to interplanetary missions, the country is steadily building a powerful ecosystem of scientific innovation. But one of the most exciting developments in recent years is not happening inside ISRO laboratories—it is unfolding inside engineering colleges and university innovation labs across India.

The ISRO Robotics Challenge – URSC 2025 (IRoC-U 2025) represents one of the most ambitious attempts to involve students directly in solving the technological problems of future planetary missions.

Organized by the U R Rao Satellite Centre (URSC) of ISRO, IRoC-U is designed to push young engineers to design robotic systems capable of operating in extreme, unknown, and GPS-denied environments—conditions very similar to Mars or the Moon.

The 2025 edition focused on a powerful theme: “Fly Me on Mars.”

The challenge: build an autonomous aerial robot capable of navigating without GPS, identifying safe landing zones, and operating in an unfamiliar terrain—exactly the type of problem that future Mars missions must solve.

Among hundreds of student teams that participated across India, one team captured national attention.

Team Galactic Gearheads from Pune Institute of Computer Technology (PICT) emerged as the overall winner of IRoC-U 2025.

Their innovative drone system demonstrated the kind of autonomy and intelligence required for the next generation of space exploration vehicles.

But the bigger story is not just about one winning team.

It is about how India is creating a new generation of space engineers capable of designing technologies that may someday explore other planets.



Space exploration today demands far more than rockets and satellites.

Modern missions require autonomous systems capable of operating independently in environments where communication delays and uncertainty are unavoidable.

For example:

• Signals from Mars can take 4 to 20 minutes to reach Earth.
• Real-time control of robots is therefore impossible.
• Machines must sense, decide, and act independently.

This is where robotics, artificial intelligence, computer vision, and autonomous navigation become essential.

ISRO launched the IRoC-U initiative to achieve several strategic goals:

1. Building a Future Space Workforce

India’s expanding space program requires thousands of highly trained engineers in robotics, AI, avionics, and control systems. Competitions like IRoC-U expose students to real mission challenges early in their careers.

2. Encouraging Deep-Tech Innovation

Rather than limiting innovation to government laboratories, IRoC-U taps into the creativity of universities, startups, and young researchers.

3. Bridging Academia and Space Agencies

By collaborating with universities, ISRO strengthens the research ecosystem and encourages applied engineering solutions rather than purely theoretical work.

4. Preparing for Future Planetary Missions

Future missions to the Moon and Mars will require aerial vehicles capable of exploring difficult terrain, such as:

• craters
• cliffs
• lava tubes
• canyon systems

These are areas where traditional rovers cannot operate effectively.

IRoC-U therefore acts as a technology incubation platform for planetary robotics.


The IRoC-U 2025 problem statement required teams to develop a fully autonomous aerial robot capable of performing several complex tasks.

Unlike consumer drones, which rely heavily on GPS for navigation, these systems had to function in GPS-denied environments, similar to Mars.

Core Requirements of the Challenge

The drone had to:

• Take off autonomously
• Map unknown terrain
• Detect obstacles
• Identify safe landing zones
• Navigate dynamically
• Return safely to its starting point

All this had to be achieved using onboard sensors, algorithms, and autonomous decision-making systems.

Teams were evaluated on several technical parameters:

• navigation accuracy
• obstacle avoidance
• terrain mapping
• system stability
• mission completion time
• innovation in design

Essentially, the competition simulated the type of autonomy required for future planetary aerial vehicles.

This approach mirrors real-world developments. For instance, NASA successfully deployed the Ingenuity helicopter on Mars, proving that aerial exploration is possible even in the planet’s extremely thin atmosphere.

ISRO’s challenge encourages Indian engineers to develop similar capabilities.

Among the many talented teams participating in IRoC-U 2025, Team Galactic Gearheads from Pune Institute of Computer Technology (PICT) stood out for their innovative engineering approach.

Their drone weighed approximately 1.7 kilograms and incorporated advanced sensing and navigation technologies.

Key Technical Features of the Winning Drone

1. LiDAR-Based Terrain Mapping

The drone used a rotating LiDAR (Light Detection and Ranging) system to create a real-time 3D map of the environment.

LiDAR works by emitting laser pulses and measuring the time taken for reflections to return. This allows the system to generate extremely accurate distance measurements.

The result is a high-resolution 3D model of the surrounding terrain.

2. Real-Time Path Planning

Using the LiDAR data, the drone continuously updated its internal map and computed the safest possible path through obstacles.

This is similar to how autonomous cars navigate urban environments.

3. Ground Server Integration

The team integrated a ground processing server to assist with computational tasks, enabling faster decision-making and better terrain analysis.

4. Autonomous Landing Zone Detection

One of the hardest problems in planetary exploration is identifying safe landing zones.

The drone’s algorithms analyzed surface flatness and obstacle distribution before selecting a suitable landing area.

5. System Stability and Reliability

The system was designed to maintain stability in challenging environments, ensuring safe flight even under unpredictable conditions.

These innovations enabled Galactic Gearheads to complete the mission successfully and secure the top position in IRoC-U 2025.

What This Means for India’s Space Future

The success of IRoC-U 2025 highlights a significant shift in how space innovation is evolving in India.

Rather than being limited to government laboratories, space technology development is gradually expanding into the broader research ecosystem.

This has several important implications.

1. Democratization of Space Innovation

Engineering students across India are now contributing ideas that may influence future missions.

2. Stronger Research Ecosystem

Universities are becoming incubators for robotics and AI research, which can benefit both space exploration and commercial industries.

3. Growth of India’s Deep-Tech Sector

Technologies developed for space missions often find applications in many other fields, such as:

• autonomous vehicles
• disaster response robots
• industrial automation
• agriculture drones
• defense systems

4. Inspiration for Future Engineers

Perhaps the most powerful outcome of IRoC-U is inspiration.

Young students watching these competitions may become the scientists and engineers who design India’s future missions to the Moon, Mars, and beyond.

The Road Ahead for IRoC-U

IRoC-U is expected to grow significantly in the coming years.

Future editions of the challenge may explore more advanced themes such as:

• lunar surface robotics
• space habitat automation
• swarm robotics for exploration
• autonomous planetary rovers
• AI-driven mission planning

As India prepares for ambitious missions such as Gaganyaan, lunar exploration programs, and potential Mars missions, the technologies developed through competitions like IRoC-U could become critical building blocks.

Conclusion: From Student Labs to Interplanetary Missions

The story of IRoC-U 2025 and Team Galactic Gearheads represents something far larger than a robotics competition.

It represents the emergence of a new generation of Indian engineers capable of solving planetary-scale problems.

By encouraging students to tackle real-world space challenges, ISRO is effectively planting the seeds for the future of Indian space exploration.

Today, these young innovators are building drones in university labs.

Tomorrow, their technologies may be flying over the canyons of Mars or the craters of the Moon.

And that is precisely the vision behind IRoC-U—transforming curiosity and creativity into the engines of India’s next great space missions.
