Glass Photonic Chip Enables Record Quantum Random Numbers and Secure Quantum Communication

What is the significance of glass-based photonic chips in quantum communication?


Glass-based photonic chips enable stable, low-loss integrated optical circuits that can perform multiple quantum communication functions, such as quantum random number generation and quantum key distribution on a single platform, supporting the development of scalable and deployable quantum networks.

Decoding Science & Technology for the Future of Secure Communication

As the world moves toward a quantum-enabled digital infrastructure, the demand for ultra-secure communication technologies is growing rapidly. Researchers have now demonstrated a breakthrough glass-based integrated photonic chip capable of performing multiple quantum communication functions on a single platform.

The device integrates advanced optical components that allow it to generate high-speed quantum random numbers and perform secure quantum key distribution (QKD) — both essential building blocks for future quantum networks.

What makes this achievement particularly significant is that the entire system is implemented on a glass photonic platform, offering advantages in stability, scalability, and deployment compared to traditional silicon photonics.

The Photonic Architecture Behind the Breakthrough

At the heart of the system lies a sophisticated photonic circuit fabricated using femtosecond laser writing, a technique that allows three-dimensional waveguides to be embedded directly inside glass.

The chip integrates several advanced optical elements that enable precise manipulation of quantum light signals:

  • Fixed and tunable beam splitters

  • Thermo-optic phase shifters for electrical control

  • Three-dimensional waveguide crossings

  • Polarization-independent directional couplers

These components allow a quantum signal and reference beam to interfere in a controlled manner, enabling simultaneous measurement of two conjugate quadratures — a fundamental requirement for continuous-variable quantum communication systems.

The device demonstrated impressive performance characteristics:

  • Ultra-low insertion loss: approximately 1 dB

  • Polarization-independent operation

  • Common-mode rejection ratio above 73 dB, enabling strong suppression of classical noise

  • Stable signal-to-noise ratio for more than 8 hours of continuous operation

Such performance meets or even exceeds that of many existing silicon-based photonic receivers, demonstrating the potential of glass photonics for quantum technologies.


Two Quantum Technologies on a Single Chip

One of the most remarkable aspects of the research is that two independent quantum communication applications were demonstrated using the same hardware platform without physical modification.

1. Quantum Random Number Generation (QRNG)

When operated as a heterodyne detector, the chip was used to demonstrate a source-device-independent quantum random number generator.

This security model ensures that the system remains secure even if the incoming optical signal cannot be trusted — an important requirement for real-world cryptographic systems.

Thanks to strong noise suppression and stable quadrature measurements, the system achieved a secure random number generation rate of 42.7 gigabits per second, setting a new record for this security framework.

Such high-speed random number generation is critical for:

  • Cryptographic key creation

  • Secure communications

  • Financial transactions

  • Defense and national security systems


2. Continuous-Variable Quantum Key Distribution (CV-QKD)

The same photonic chip was also used to implement a QPSK-based continuous-variable quantum key distribution protocol.

In this approach, information is encoded in the quantum states of light using a four-state quantum constellation, enabling highly secure data transmission.

During testing over a simulated 9.3-kilometer fiber link, the system achieved:

Secure key generation rate: 3.2 Mbit/s

These results demonstrate that a glass-based photonic interface can deliver state-of-the-art QKD performance, rivaling conventional silicon photonic systems while avoiding some of their limitations.

Why Glass Photonics Matters

Beyond raw performance, the research highlights several important advantages of glass as a platform for integrated quantum photonics.

Environmental Stability

Glass is chemically inert and resistant to thermal and mechanical fluctuations, making it highly reliable for long-term operation.

Low-Loss Fiber Coupling

The waveguide dimensions in glass closely match those of standard telecommunications optical fibers, minimizing signal loss during coupling.

True 3D Circuit Design

Unlike silicon photonics, femtosecond laser writing allows three-dimensional waveguide structures, enabling complex routing and waveguide crossings without additional scattering losses.

Faster and More Affordable Fabrication

Glass photonic circuits can be produced without expensive semiconductor manufacturing steps, enabling rapid prototyping and potentially lower production costs.

Toward Real-World Quantum Networks

These characteristics make glass photonic technology especially promising for deployable quantum communication systems.

The researchers suggest that such devices could eventually support:

  • Long-distance quantum communication networks

  • Secure financial and government communications

  • Quantum-safe internet infrastructure

  • Satellite-based quantum communication systems

By combining low optical loss, electrical tunability, and long-term stability, the glass-based photonic platform could help bridge the gap between laboratory-scale quantum experiments and real-world quantum communication infrastructure.

The Road Ahead

As quantum computing advances, traditional cryptographic methods may become vulnerable to future quantum attacks. Technologies such as quantum key distribution and quantum random number generation are emerging as critical tools to secure the next generation of digital communication.

This new glass photonic chip demonstrates that integrated quantum photonics can deliver high performance while remaining scalable, stable, and deployable.

If further developed, such technologies could form the backbone of the future quantum internet.
