 Microscopic Spotlight: The Ultra-Thin Chip That Can Control Light


Imagine a device thinner than a human hair that can change the color of light and direct it precisely like a spotlight. Scientists at the Advanced Science Research Center at the CUNY Graduate Center have now demonstrated such a breakthrough using a nanoscale metasurface chip.

A metasurface chip developed by researchers at the CUNY Advanced Science Research Center can convert infrared light into visible light and steer it into different directions without moving parts. The ultra-thin device uses nanoscale structures to generate third-harmonic light and control its direction, enabling compact technologies for LiDAR, quantum optics, and optical communication.

Imagine a device thinner than a human hair that can change the color of light and direct it precisely like a spotlight.

A team of researchers at the Advanced Science Research Center (ASRC) at the CUNY Graduate Center has demonstrated such a device using a specially engineered metasurface — an ultra-thin optical structure that manipulates light at the nanoscale.

Their breakthrough, reported in the journal eLight, shows that a flat chip can convert invisible infrared light into visible green light while simultaneously steering the beam in different directions.

The innovation could enable a new generation of compact optical technologies for communication, sensing, and quantum computing.

What Is a Metasurface?

Metasurfaces are engineered materials made of tiny patterned structures smaller than the wavelength of light.

Unlike traditional optical components such as lenses or mirrors, which rely on bulk materials, metasurfaces manipulate light using nanoscale geometry.

By precisely designing these patterns, scientists can control:

• The direction of light
• The phase and polarization
• The color (frequency) of light

This makes metasurfaces one of the most promising platforms for next-generation photonics.

What makes metasurfaces truly revolutionary is their ability to replace bulky optical systems with ultra-thin, flat components. This not only reduces size and weight but also enables integration into compact devices like smartphones, wearable tech, and advanced sensors. As industries move toward miniaturization, metasurfaces are expected to become a foundational technology in future optical engineering.

Turning Infrared Into Visible Light

In their experiment, researchers illuminated the metasurface with an infrared laser at about 1530 nanometers, a wavelength commonly used in fiber-optic communication systems.

The device then generated visible green light at around 510 nanometers.

This transformation occurs through a nonlinear optical process known as third-harmonic generation, where the frequency of light is multiplied by three.

Even more remarkably, the outgoing green light forms a narrow beam that can be directed toward specific angles.

Changing the polarization of the incoming infrared light automatically changes the direction of the beam.

In effect, the chip acts as a microscopic spotlight with programmable direction.

This capability is particularly significant because it combines two critical optical functions—frequency conversion and beam steering—into a single device. Traditionally, these tasks required separate bulky components. By integrating both into a nanoscale chip, researchers are paving the way for more efficient and compact optical systems, especially in telecommunications and advanced imaging technologies.

Solving a Major Metasurface Engineering Problem

For years, metasurface design faced a fundamental limitation.

Two approaches existed:

Local Control Structures

Each tiny pixel controls light individually.

✔ High precision
❌ Low efficiency

Collective Resonance Structures

Light interacts across the entire surface.

✔ High efficiency
❌ Poor directional control

The new device successfully combines both advantages.

It uses a phenomenon known as a quasi-bound state in the continuum (QBIC).

This resonance traps incoming light across the entire surface, dramatically increasing the intensity of the optical interaction.

At the same time, the researchers rotated each nanoscale element according to a carefully engineered pattern, enabling precise beam shaping.

The result is a device that delivers both efficiency and control.

This hybrid approach represents a major engineering milestone because it resolves a long-standing trade-off in photonics. By enabling both high efficiency and precise control, the technology becomes viable for real-world applications. It also demonstrates how advanced physics concepts like QBIC can translate into practical innovations, bridging the gap between theoretical research and industry deployment.

100× Efficiency Improvement

Because of this hybrid design, the metasurface produces third-harmonic signals that are about 100 times stronger than comparable beam-shaping devices without collective resonance.

This dramatic increase in efficiency is essential for practical applications in integrated photonic systems.

Efficiency is a critical factor in scaling any technology from laboratory experiments to commercial products. A 100× improvement means lower energy consumption, stronger signal output, and better reliability. This breakthrough significantly enhances the feasibility of deploying metasurfaces in everyday technologies, from consumer electronics to industrial sensing systems.


Future Applications of Metasurface Light Control

The ability to convert and steer light using a single flat chip opens exciting possibilities.

Potential applications include:

LiDAR Systems

Compact beam-steering devices for autonomous vehicles and robotics.

Quantum Photonics

On-chip generation of quantum light sources for secure communication.

Optical Signal Processing

Advanced photonic circuits that manipulate data using light instead of electrons.

Spectroscopy and Sensors

Miniaturized devices capable of detecting chemical signatures with high precision.

The versatility of metasurfaces means their impact could span multiple industries simultaneously. From enabling safer autonomous vehicles to enhancing secure communication networks, this technology has the potential to redefine how we use light in computing and sensing. Its adaptability also ensures that it can evolve alongside emerging technological demands.

Toward Integrated Photonic Chips

Researchers also believe that stacking multiple metasurfaces could allow the device to operate across broader wavelength ranges.

Because the design principle depends primarily on geometry rather than specific materials, the concept could be applied to different nonlinear materials and optical frequencies — including ultraviolet light.

This flexibility could accelerate the development of fully integrated photonic chips, where light sources, detectors, and beam steering systems are all embedded on a single platform.

Integrated photonic chips represent the next evolution in computing hardware, where light replaces electrons for faster and more efficient processing. By enabling multiple optical functions on a single chip, metasurfaces could drastically reduce system complexity while improving performance, opening new frontiers in computing, communication, and sensing technologies.



A Glimpse Into the Future of Light Engineering

The metasurface chip represents a step toward a future where optical components become flat, programmable, and incredibly compact.

Instead of bulky lenses and moving mirrors, tomorrow’s optical technologies may rely on nanostructured surfaces that control light with extreme precision.

In essence, researchers are learning to engineer light itself.

And that ability could reshape technologies ranging from autonomous sensing to quantum computing and ultra-fast communications.

This breakthrough is more than just a scientific achievement—it signals a paradigm shift in how we interact with light. As researchers continue refining these technologies, we may soon see a world where optical systems are seamlessly embedded into everyday devices, transforming industries and redefining the boundaries of innovation.
