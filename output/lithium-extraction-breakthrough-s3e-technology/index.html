Scientists Develop Faster and Cheaper Method to Extract Lithium From Brines

👤 K M Manohar | Independent Science and Technology Analyst  |  📅 2026-03-16

 

Introduction - 

Lithium has become one of the most strategically important elements in the modern world. 


Often referred to as the “white gold” of the clean energy transition, lithium is essential for rechargeable batteries used in electric vehicles, smartphones, laptops, and large-scale renewable energy storage systems.

As countries accelerate their shift toward electrification and renewable power, global demand for lithium is rising at an unprecedented pace. However, the current methods used to extract lithium are slow, environmentally intensive, and geographically limited.

A team of researchers from Columbia Engineering has now developed a promising alternative that could dramatically reshape lithium production worldwide. Their innovation, known as Switchable Solvent Selective Extraction (S3E), uses a temperature-sensitive solvent to recover lithium directly from brines — potentially making extraction faster, cheaper, and far more sustainable.

The Growing Global Demand for Lithium

Lithium demand is exploding due to several converging technological and economic trends:

• Rapid growth of electric vehicles (EVs)
• Expansion of renewable energy storage systems
• Rising demand for consumer electronics
• Government mandates pushing electrification

Battery production alone is expected to drive lithium demand five to ten times higher over the next two decades.

However, expanding supply fast enough remains a major challenge.

Why Current Lithium Extraction Methods Are Problematic

Today, lithium is primarily produced using two methods:

1. Solar Evaporation from Brines

Nearly 40% of global lithium production comes from underground salty water reservoirs called brines.

In this process:

  1. Brine is pumped to the surface.

  2. It is stored in massive evaporation ponds.

  3. Water evaporates under the sun for months or even years.

  4. Lithium concentration gradually increases.

This technique is used extensively in places like the Atacama Desert in Chile and parts of Nevada in the United States.

However, the process has serious drawbacks:

• Requires huge land areas
• Consumes significant water resources
• Can take up to two years to produce lithium

2. Hard-Rock Mining

Lithium can also be extracted from minerals such as spodumene through mining and chemical processing.

But this method is:

• Energy intensive
• Expensive
• Environmentally disruptive

The Science Behind S3E Technology


The new approach developed by Columbia researchers is called Switchable Solvent Selective Extraction (S3E).

Unlike conventional extraction methods, S3E does not rely on large evaporation ponds or complex chemical binding agents.

Instead, the process exploits the interaction between lithium ions and water molecules within a temperature-sensitive solvent.

The process works in two key stages:

Stage 1: Extraction

At room temperature:

• The solvent pulls lithium and water molecules from the brine.
• Other ions, such as sodium and potassium, remain largely behind.

Stage 2: Release

When the solvent is heated:

• Lithium and water are released into a purified solution.
• The solvent regenerates itself and can be reused.

This reversible behavior allows the process to operate in cycles, potentially enabling continuous lithium recovery systems.

Laboratory Results and Performance

Researchers tested the technology using synthetic brines modeled after the Salton Sea geothermal region in California, a location believed to contain enough lithium to supply more than 375 million electric vehicle batteries.

The results were promising.

Key findings include:

• Nearly 40% lithium recovery after four extraction cycles
• Lithium selectivity 10× higher than sodium
• 12× higher selectivity than potassium
• Effective removal of magnesium contamination

Magnesium is a particularly difficult contaminant in lithium brines, and removing it is often one of the most expensive steps in lithium processing.

The new method triggers chemical precipitation, allowing magnesium to separate naturally from the solution.

The potential environmental benefits of S3E are significant.

Compared with conventional lithium extraction, the technology could:

✔ Reduce land use
✔ Minimize water consumption
✔ Avoid large evaporation ponds
✔ Use low-grade heat from waste industrial processes or solar collectors

This could dramatically reduce the environmental footprint of lithium production.

As researcher Ngai Yin Yip noted:

“If we want a truly sustainable energy transition, we need cleaner ways to obtain the materials it depends on.”

Potential Impact on the Global Lithium Supply Chain

If scaled successfully, S3E could unlock lithium resources previously considered inaccessible.

Many lithium-rich brines around the world contain:

• Low lithium concentrations
• High contamination levels
• Unfavorable climates for evaporation

These factors currently make them uneconomical.

The new technology could allow extraction from:

• Geothermal brines
• Oilfield brines
• Industrial wastewater streams

This would dramatically expand the global lithium supply base.

Challenges Before Commercial Deployment

Despite its promise, S3E is still in the proof-of-concept stage.

Several hurdles remain before commercialization:

• Optimizing solvent efficiency
• Improving lithium recovery yield
• Engineering large-scale continuous systems
• Reducing operational costs

However, early results suggest that the technology could become a major component of next-generation lithium extraction systems.

The Future of Sustainable Lithium Extraction

As the global energy transition accelerates, the demand for battery materials will only continue to grow.

Lithium sits at the center of this transformation.

But the future of clean energy must also address the environmental costs of mining the materials it relies on.

Technologies like S3E represent a crucial step toward a more sustainable resource economy — one where the materials powering green energy are themselves produced in greener ways.

If innovations like this reach commercial scale, they could reshape the entire lithium supply chain and accelerate the global shift toward electrification.
