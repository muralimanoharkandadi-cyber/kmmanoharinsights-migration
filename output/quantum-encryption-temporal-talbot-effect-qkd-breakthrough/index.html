4. What is Quantum Key Distribution (QKD)?

Quantum Key Distribution is a secure communication method that uses single photons to generate encryption keys.

Key Advantage:
Any attempt to intercept the key disturbs the quantum state—alerting both parties instantly.

5. Limitations of Traditional QKD

Most existing QKD systems rely on qubits, which allow only two states:

  • 0 or 1

  • Early or Late photon

Problem:

  • Limited data capacity

  • Complex detection systems

  • Difficult scalability

6. The Temporal Talbot Effect Explained


Originally discovered by Henry Fox Talbot in 1836, this effect shows how light patterns repeat after passing through a grating.

In quantum systems:

  • Light pulses act like a “grating.”

  • Optical fibers introduce dispersion

  • Pulses reconstruct over time

  • Phase differences reveal encoded information

7. High-Dimensional Quantum Encoding

Instead of simple qubits, researchers used multi-dimensional quantum states:

Example:

  • 2D → 2 states

  • 4D → 4 states

  • Future → even higher dimensions

Benefits:

  • More data per photon

  • Increased efficiency

  • Enhanced encryption strength

8. Breakthrough Experimental Setup


Key Innovation:

  • Eliminates complex interferometer trees

  • Works across multiple dimensions without hardware changes

Result:

  • Lower cost

  • Practical deployment potential

9. Real-World Testing in Urban Networks

The system was tested:

10. Security Challenges and Solutions
