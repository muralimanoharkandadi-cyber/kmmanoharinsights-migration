Project Kusha: India’s Next-Gen Air Defence Shield to Redefine National Security and Strategic Leap Beyond the S-400.

👤 K M Manohar | Independent Science and Technology Analyst  |  📅 2026-03-01

Project Kusha: India’s Sky Shield Revolution

The Indigenous Air Defence System That Could Transform India’s Strategic Security


For decades, India’s air defence architecture relied heavily on imported missile systems. From Soviet-era surface-to-air missiles to modern acquisitions like the Russian S-400 Triumf, foreign technology has played a crucial role in safeguarding Indian airspace.

However, India’s rapidly evolving security environment—marked by advanced Chinese missile systems, stealth aircraft, drone warfare, and hypersonic weapons—has forced policymakers to rethink long-term defence strategy. Dependence on external suppliers creates strategic vulnerabilities, particularly during geopolitical crises.

This is where Project Kusha emerges as a landmark initiative.

Approved by the Indian government in 2022 with an estimated outlay of ₹21,700 crore, Project Kusha aims to build an entirely indigenous, next-generation long-range air defence system capable of rivaling the world’s most advanced missile shields. Led by the Defence Research and Development Organisation (DRDO) and supported by Bharat Electronics Limited (BEL) and several Indian defence firms, the program represents a major milestone in India’s Atmanirbhar Bharat (self-reliant India) defence strategy.

If successful, Kusha could become the backbone of India’s future air defence grid—capable of neutralizing threats ranging from stealth aircraft and drones to hypersonic missiles.


India’s air defence strategy has evolved through several phases since independence.

Early Phase: Soviet Systems

During the Cold War era, India relied on Soviet-origin systems such as:

  • SA-2

  • SA-3

  • SA-6

These systems provided basic defence against aircraft but were limited against modern missiles and electronic warfare.

Modernization Phase

In the 2000s, India began upgrading its air defence capabilities through:

  • Akash surface-to-air missile systems

  • Barak-8 / MR-SAM systems developed jointly with Israel

  • Upgrades to radar and early warning networks

Strategic Leap: S-400 Acquisition

India’s purchase of the S-400 Triumf system from Russia marked a major technological upgrade. The S-400 provides interception ranges of up to 400 km and the ability to target aircraft, cruise missiles, and ballistic missiles.

However, reliance on imports comes with several risks:

  • Geopolitical pressure and sanctions

  • Supply chain dependency

  • Limited technology transfer

Project Kusha aims to eliminate these constraints by creating a fully indigenous long-range air defence ecosystem.

What Exactly Is Project Kusha?

Project Kusha is an advanced multi-layered air defence system designed to protect large areas of Indian airspace against a wide spectrum of aerial threats.

The program is being led by several key DRDO laboratories:

  • DRDL (Defence Research and Development Laboratory) – missile development

  • RCI (Research Centre Imarat) – advanced seekers and avionics

  • LRDE (Electronics & Radar Development Establishment) – radar systems

Production and system integration will involve Bharat Electronics Limited (BEL) and multiple private defence manufacturers.

The goal is to create a system comparable to advanced international platforms such as:

  • Russia’s S-400

  • Russia’s S-500

  • U.S. Patriot PAC-3

  • Israel’s Arrow missile defence system

But at significantly lower cost and with full domestic control.



Project Kusha is expected to rely on several advanced technologies that together form a layered defence architecture.

1. Multi-Layered Interceptor System

Kusha will deploy multiple interceptor missiles, each optimized for different ranges and threat types.

The key interceptor families are expected to include:

M1 Interceptor

Short-to-medium range defence against:

  • drones

  • cruise missiles

  • fighter aircraft

M2 Interceptor

Medium-to-long range interceptor designed to neutralize:

  • aircraft

  • ballistic missiles

  • anti-ship missiles

M3 Interceptor

The most advanced interceptor in the system.

Capabilities expected:

  • interception range up to 350–400 km

  • engagement of high-speed ballistic threats

  • capability to intercept Mach 7 missiles

This places Kusha in the same league as long-range global air defence systems.

2. Advanced Radar Systems

Detection is the foundation of modern air defence.

Project Kusha will rely on long-range AESA (Active Electronically Scanned Array) radar systems, capable of:

  • detecting stealth aircraft

  • tracking multiple targets simultaneously

  • operating in high electronic warfare environments

These radars will be developed primarily by BEL and DRDO laboratories, ensuring domestic control of critical technologies.

3. AI-Enabled Battle Management

Modern warfare involves hundreds of simultaneous aerial threats, from drones to ballistic missiles.

Project Kusha integrates AI-driven battle management systems capable of:

  • real-time threat prioritization

  • automated interceptor assignment

  • dynamic engagement strategies

This dramatically reduces response time during missile attacks.

4. Integration with India’s IACCS Network

India already operates a powerful digital command network known as the Integrated Air Command and Control System (IACCS).

Kusha will seamlessly integrate into this network, allowing:

  • coordinated responses across multiple air defence units

  • data sharing between radars, fighters, and missile systems

  • centralized command and decentralized engagement

This integration creates a nationwide defensive grid.

Strategic Importance of Project Kusha



Project Kusha carries immense strategic implications for India.

1. Countering Emerging Threats

India faces increasingly complex aerial threats from both China and Pakistan.

These include:

  • stealth fighter aircraft

  • long-range cruise missiles

  • drone swarms

  • hypersonic weapons

Kusha is designed to counter all of these threat vectors simultaneously, creating a robust defence shield.

2. Strategic Autonomy

One of the biggest advantages of Kusha is technological sovereignty.

Indigenous development ensures:

  • freedom from export restrictions

  • uninterrupted upgrades

  • complete operational control

This aligns with India’s long-term goal of becoming a global defence technology power.

3. Cost Advantage

Foreign air defence systems are extremely expensive.

For comparison:

  • One S-400 squadron costs several billion dollars.

  • Maintenance and upgrades require foreign support.

Project Kusha’s ₹21,700 crore budget for five squadrons suggests that India can deploy an advanced system at less than half the cost of comparable imports.

This cost efficiency enables wider deployment across strategic locations.

4. Strengthening the Domestic Defence Industry

The project will boost India’s defence ecosystem.

Key beneficiaries include:

  • Bharat Electronics Limited

  • Bharat Dynamics Limited

  • Larsen & Toubro Defence Division

  • private aerospace manufacturers

Large defence programs like Kusha create high-technology supply chains, stimulating innovation in electronics, radar engineering, and missile propulsion.

Development Timeline

Project Kusha is expected to progress in several phases.

2022

Government approval and funding allocation.

2024–2025

Initial design and subsystem development.

2026–2027

Prototype testing and radar validation.

2028–2029

Interceptor trials.

Early 2030s

Deployment of the first operational squadrons.

Once deployed, Kusha will likely become a central pillar of India’s air defence grid.

Future Potential: Beyond 400 km

The ambitions behind Project Kusha extend beyond current specifications.

Future upgrades could include:

  • anti-hypersonic interceptors

  • space-based tracking integration

  • longer interception ranges exceeding 500 km

  • directed energy weapons

If these upgrades materialize, India could join the elite group of nations with a multi-domain missile defence capability.

Geopolitical Implications

Project Kusha is also strategically important in the context of the Indo-Pacific security environment.

China has rapidly expanded its missile capabilities, including:

  • DF-17 hypersonic glide vehicles

  • long-range cruise missiles

  • advanced stealth fighters

Pakistan has also enhanced its missile inventory with new systems and drone warfare capabilities.

A robust air defence system like Kusha strengthens India’s deterrence posture, reducing the effectiveness of adversarial missile strategies.

Challenges Ahead

Despite its promise, Project Kusha faces several challenges.

Technological Complexity

Developing advanced interceptors and radars is extremely complex and requires sustained funding and testing.

Hypersonic Threats

Intercepting hypersonic weapons remains one of the hardest problems in defence technology.

Production Scaling

Manufacturing multiple squadrons quickly requires large industrial capacity.

However, India’s experience with programs like Akash, BrahMos, and Ballistic Missile Defence (BMD) provides a strong technological foundation.

Conclusion: A Turning Point in India’s Defence Technology

Project Kusha represents more than just another missile system.

It symbolizes India’s transition from a defence importer to a defence innovator.

By combining indigenous missile interceptors, advanced radar networks, AI-driven battle management, and integration with national command systems, Kusha could transform India’s air defence landscape.

If executed successfully, the program will:

  • enhance national security

  • strengthen strategic autonomy

  • Accelerate domestic defence manufacturing

  • Position India among the world’s leading missile defence innovators

In an era defined by rapidly evolving aerial threats, Project Kusha may well become India’s ultimate sky shield—protecting the nation while showcasing the power of indigenous technological ambition.
