Beyond the Hype: Why Indian Avionics Could Redefine the Su-57 vs F-35 Debate.

 Su-57 vs F-35: How Indian Avionics and GaN... Radar Technology Could Redefine Air Superiority




For decades, the balance of global air power has been shaped by the technological race among major military powers. From the Cold War era dogfights between Soviet and Western fighters to today’s stealth-dominated battlefield, fighter aircraft have evolved into highly sophisticated systems where electronics, sensors, and software often matter more than raw speed or maneuverability.

The ongoing discussion surrounding the Russian Su-57 fifth-generation fighter and the American F-35 Lightning II highlights this transformation. While both aircraft are considered advanced combat platforms, the debate has recently gained new momentum with the possibility of integrating Indian-developed avionics and Gallium Nitride (GaN) based AESA radar systems.

Such integration could potentially reshape how modern air combat is fought, not by simply comparing aircraft platforms, but by emphasizing the role of advanced electronics, sensor fusion, and indigenous technological capabilities.

The Evolution of Air Combat: From Speed to Sensors

Historically, air superiority depended on aircraft speed, maneuverability, and weapons payload. Early jet fighters relied on pilots’ visual range combat abilities and radar systems that were relatively primitive.

However, modern warfare has shifted dramatically toward information dominance.

Today’s combat aircraft function as airborne data centers equipped with:

  • Advanced radar systems

  • Electronic warfare suites

  • Network-centric communication systems

  • Artificial intelligence-assisted targeting

  • Sensor fusion technologies

In this new environment, the aircraft that detects its enemy first usually wins the engagement.

Stealth technology, advanced radars, and powerful electronic warfare systems have therefore become central to fifth-generation aircraft design.

The F-35 Lightning II represents the United States’ most ambitious fighter aircraft program. Designed as a multi-role stealth aircraft, the F-35 integrates cutting-edge technologies that enable it to perform a wide range of missions, including air superiority, ground attack, intelligence gathering, and electronic warfare.

One of the defining features of the F-35 is its sensor fusion capability. Multiple onboard sensors—including radar, infrared search and track systems, and electronic intelligence receivers—combine data to create a comprehensive situational awareness picture for the pilot.

This allows the pilot to view a unified battlefield image rather than interpreting individual sensor inputs.

The F-35 also operates as part of a network-centric warfare system, sharing data with other aircraft, satellites, and ground forces in real time.

However, the aircraft’s design philosophy emphasizes stealth above all else. Its shape, materials, and internal weapons bays reduce radar visibility, enabling it to operate undetected in hostile airspace.

Despite these advantages, critics argue that the F-35’s reliance on stealth may face increasing challenges as radar technologies continue to evolve.



The Sukhoi Su-57 represents Russia’s attempt to develop a fighter capable of competing with Western fifth-generation aircraft.

Unlike the F-35, which prioritizes stealth and sensor integration, the Su-57 follows a different design philosophy. Russian engineers sought to combine stealth characteristics with supermaneuverability, long-range weapons capability, and powerful radar systems.

Key features of the Su-57 include:

  • Advanced aerodynamic design

  • Thrust-vectoring engines

  • Distributed radar arrays

  • Long-range missile capability

  • High agility in dogfighting scenarios

The aircraft is equipped with multiple radar systems, including the N036 Byelka AESA radar, which includes several antenna arrays positioned across the aircraft’s fuselage.

This distributed radar architecture allows the Su-57 to detect threats from multiple angles, potentially improving situational awareness.

However, some analysts argue that the aircraft’s avionics and electronics could benefit significantly from modernization and integration with advanced semiconductor technologies.


Radar technology has undergone a dramatic transformation over the past two decades.

Earlier radar systems used mechanically rotating antennas. These radars were limited in speed, reliability, and tracking capability.

Modern fighters now use Active Electronically Scanned Array (AESA) radars.

Instead of a single transmitter, AESA radars use hundreds or thousands of small transmit-receive modules that electronically steer radar beams.

This allows for:

  • Faster target detection

  • Simultaneous tracking of multiple targets

  • Improved resistance to jamming

  • Higher reliability

Even more important is the shift toward Gallium Nitride (GaN) semiconductor technology.

Compared with older Gallium Arsenide (GaAs) radar modules, GaN provides:

  • Higher power output

  • Greater efficiency

  • Increased radar detection range

  • Better heat resistance

  • Stronger electronic warfare capability

These advantages make GaN radars extremely attractive for next-generation fighter aircraft.

India’s Growing Capabilities in Defence Electronics

Over the past decade, India has significantly expanded its capabilities in defence electronics and radar development.

Organizations such as the Defence Research and Development Organisation (DRDO) and Indian defence industry partners have been working on several advanced radar systems designed for fighter aircraft and air defense networks.

Among these developments is the Virupaksha AESA radar, an advanced radar system designed to incorporate GaN semiconductor technology.

Such radar systems aim to provide:

  • Enhanced target detection range

  • Improved tracking of stealth aircraft

  • Better resistance against electronic countermeasures

  • High reliability in combat conditions

India’s growing expertise in semiconductor technology and radar design reflects a broader national strategy of achieving strategic technological autonomy.

The goal is not only to build indigenous weapons systems but also to develop the electronic architecture that powers them.

The Concept of Integrating Indian Avionics

The idea of integrating Indian avionics into advanced fighter aircraft platforms has gained increasing attention in defense discussions.

If advanced GaN AESA radar systems and indigenous avionics suites were integrated into aircraft such as the Su-57, the resulting platform could benefit from both aerodynamic design and modern electronic architecture.

Such integration could potentially provide several advantages:

Greater Customization

Indian avionics systems can be tailored for specific mission requirements, allowing upgrades and modifications without external restrictions.

Software Sovereignty

Control over mission software eliminates the possibility of foreign control mechanisms or limitations.

Faster Upgrade Cycles

Domestic development allows faster upgrades as new technologies emerge.

Improved Electronic Warfare Capability

Indigenous systems can be designed specifically to counter regional threats and enemy radar systems.

Stealth vs Detection: The Future Battlefield

One of the most important debates in modern military aviation revolves around the future of stealth technology.

While stealth reduces the radar cross-section of aircraft, it does not make them invisible.

Advanced radar technologies—including multi-band radar systems and GaN-based AESA radars—are increasingly capable of detecting stealth aircraft under certain conditions.

This evolving dynamic suggests that the future of air combat will depend on the interaction between:

  • Stealth technology

  • Radar detection capability

  • Electronic warfare systems

  • Artificial intelligence-assisted targeting

In such an environment, electronics may ultimately determine the outcome of aerial engagements.

The Rise of Software-Defined Warfare

Modern military platforms increasingly rely on software as their core operational layer.

From radar algorithms to electronic warfare tactics, software determines how effectively hardware systems operate.

This shift has given rise to the concept of software-defined warfare, where upgrades can be deployed digitally rather than through physical modifications.

For countries seeking strategic independence, controlling the software stack of military platforms becomes critically important.

India’s emphasis on indigenous avionics reflects this broader transformation.

Strategic Implications for Global Air Power

The potential integration of advanced avionics and radar systems into fighter aircraft platforms has implications far beyond individual aircraft comparisons.

It reflects a larger shift in global military technology trends.

Several key developments are shaping this new era:

  • Rapid advances in semiconductor technology

  • Increasing importance of electronic warfare

  • Network-centric battle management systems

  • Artificial intelligence in military decision making

  • Indigenous technology development by emerging powers

Countries that control these technologies will likely shape the future balance of power in air warfare.

Conclusion: Technology Will Decide the Next Air War

The debate surrounding the Su-57 and F-35 highlights an important truth about modern warfare.

Air superiority is no longer determined solely by aircraft design or engine power.

Instead, the outcome increasingly depends on electronics, radar systems, software integration, and technological independence.

Advances in GaN radar technology, AESA systems, and indigenous avionics development demonstrate how rapidly the landscape of military aviation is evolving.

For emerging technology powers like India, mastering these capabilities represents not just a military advantage but also a step toward greater strategic autonomy.

In the coming decades, the aircraft that dominates the skies may not simply be the one with the best stealth or the fastest engines.

It will likely be the aircraft equipped with the most intelligent sensors, the most resilient electronics, and the most adaptable software systems.

And in that contest, the race has only just begun.
