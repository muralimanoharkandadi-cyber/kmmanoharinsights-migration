China’s Dominance, Global Supply Chain Wars, and India’s Strategic Opportunity

The global competition for Rare Earth Elements (REEs) has entered a decisive phase. What once appeared to be a niche mining sector has now become one of the most strategic battlegrounds in geopolitics, technology, and industrial policy.

From electric vehicles and wind turbines to missile guidance systems and artificial intelligence infrastructure, rare earth elements are essential to the technologies defining the 21st century.

For decades, one country quietly built overwhelming control over this critical resource ecosystem.

That country is China.

Today, China controls approximately 70% of global rare earth mining and nearly 90% of the processing and refining capacity, giving it unprecedented leverage over global technology supply chains.

However, as the world enters 2026, the rare earth industry is undergoing a profound transformation. Governments, technology companies, and defense sectors are now racing to reduce dependence on a single supply source.

At the same time, countries like India are beginning to recognize the strategic opportunity hidden within their own rare earth resources.

Understanding this shift requires examining how China built its dominance, how the rest of the world is responding, and where India fits into the emerging global supply chain.


Despite the name, rare earth elements are not truly rare in terms of geological availability. Many countries possess deposits of these minerals. The challenge lies not in finding them, but in extracting, separating, and refining them economically.

Rare earth elements consist of 17 metallic elements, including neodymium, praseodymium, dysprosium, and terbium. These materials possess unique magnetic, luminescent, and electrochemical properties that cannot easily be replaced.

Their importance spans multiple industries:

Electric Vehicles

Permanent magnets used in EV motors rely heavily on neodymium-iron-boron magnets, which contain rare earth metals.

Renewable Energy

Offshore wind turbines require powerful permanent magnets containing dysprosium and terbium.

Defense Technology

Rare earths are essential in:

  • missile guidance systems

  • fighter jet electronics

  • radar systems

  • satellite communications

Artificial Intelligence and Data Centers

High-performance computing infrastructure uses rare earth components in cooling systems, sensors, and electronics.

As global electrification accelerates, demand for rare earth elements is expected to triple by 2035, making them one of the most strategic mineral resources of the modern era.

China’s dominance over rare earth supply chains was not accidental. It was the result of a carefully planned industrial strategy spanning more than four decades.

Beginning in the 1980s, China recognized that rare earth elements could become strategically important for future technologies.

The country adopted a “mine-to-magnet” strategy, focusing on three key pillars:

1. Aggressive Mining Expansion

China invested heavily in rare earth mining, particularly in Inner Mongolia's Bayan Obo deposit, which remains one of the largest rare earth reserves in the world.

Government subsidies enabled Chinese producers to supply rare earth minerals at extremely low prices, forcing competitors in the United States and elsewhere to shut down operations.

By the early 2000s, China had effectively eliminated most global competition.

2. Mastery of Processing Technology

Mining rare earth ores is only the first step. The real challenge lies in separating the individual elements through complex chemical processes, especially solvent extraction.

China invested billions in refining technology and built a massive intellectual property portfolio. Today, Chinese companies hold more than 25,000 patents related to rare earth processing technologies.

This created a powerful reality:

Even if other countries mined rare earth ores, they often had to send them to China for refining.

3. Vertical Integration

China did not stop at mining and refining. It moved further downstream into magnet manufacturing, electronics, and electric vehicle supply chains.

This created a vertically integrated ecosystem where rare earth materials flowed seamlessly from mines to finished products.

The result is that China currently produces around 90% of the world's high-performance rare earth magnets, a critical bottleneck in global supply chains.

In recent years, China’s approach to rare earths has evolved from industrial dominance to active market management.

Several developments between 2025 and early 2026 illustrate this shift.

Export Controls

China has expanded export restrictions beyond raw rare earth materials to include processing technologies and specialized equipment.

This move effectively limits the ability of other countries to replicate China’s refining capabilities.

Strategic Price Management

In early 2026, Chinese authorities began setting quarterly benchmark prices for rare earth concentrates, stabilizing domestic supply while global prices fluctuate.

This approach allows Chinese manufacturers to maintain a cost advantage over international competitors, especially startups trying to build new supply chains.

The Magnet Bottleneck

While mining diversification has begun, the manufacturing of high-performance magnets remains concentrated in China.

These magnets are essential for:

  • electric vehicles

  • robotics

  • renewable energy systems

  • advanced military equipment

As a result, rare earth supply chains have become a critical geopolitical tool.

The Global Counter-Offensive

Recognizing the risks of dependency, major economies have launched efforts to build alternative rare earth supply chains.

United States

The U.S. has revived domestic mining through companies such as MP Materials, which operates the Mountain Pass mine in California.

The U.S. government is also investing heavily in domestic refining and magnet production.

Australia

Australia has become one of the most important alternative suppliers through Lynas Rare Earths, which operates both mining and refining facilities.

The country is also partnering with the United States and Japan to strengthen supply chains.

Europe

The European Union has introduced the Critical Raw Materials Act, aiming to reduce dependence on external suppliers for strategic minerals.

However, despite these initiatives, forecasts suggest that China could still control around 70–75% of global rare earth refining by 2030.

Technology Solutions: Thrifting and Recycling

Another strategy to reduce dependence on rare earths involves reducing the amount required in manufacturing.

Companies like Apple and Tesla are investing in magnet technologies that use fewer heavy rare-earth elements.

One such technique is grain-boundary diffusion, which improves magnet performance while reducing the need for scarce materials like dysprosium.

Recycling is also becoming increasingly important. Rare earth magnets from old electronics, wind turbines, and electric vehicles can be recovered and reused.

However, large-scale recycling infrastructure remains in its early stages.

India’s Strategic Position in the Rare Earth Landscape

India possesses a unique opportunity in the rare earth sector.

The country holds the fifth-largest rare earth reserves in the world, particularly in monazite-rich beach sands along the eastern and southern coasts.

For decades, India’s rare earth sector remained largely under government control through IREL (India) Limited.

However, recent policy shifts suggest a more ambitious strategy.

Critical Minerals Mission

India has launched a national initiative to secure supply chains for critical minerals, including rare earth elements.

This initiative focuses on:

  • exploration of domestic deposits

  • overseas mineral acquisitions

  • development of refining capacity

Private Sector Participation

The government has begun opening the rare earth sector to private companies and international partnerships, a major change from earlier restrictions.

Linking Rare Earths to Strategic Industries

India’s growing capabilities in electric vehicles, renewable energy, and defense manufacturing create strong domestic demand for rare earth materials.

If India successfully builds refining and magnet manufacturing capacity, it could emerge as a key player in the global supply chain.

The Future of Rare Earth Geopolitics

Rare earth elements are rapidly becoming one of the defining strategic resources of the modern world.

The competition is no longer about who has mineral deposits underground.

Instead, the true battle is over:

  • processing technologies

  • intellectual property

  • manufacturing ecosystems

  • integrated supply chains

China’s decades-long strategy has given it an enormous lead.

But the rest of the world is beginning to respond.

For India, the next decade presents a rare opportunity. By combining its natural resources, technological talent, and industrial ambitions, India could transform itself from a resource holder into a strategic supplier of critical minerals.

In a world increasingly defined by technological competition, rare earth supply chains may become as important as oil once was.

The countries that master them will shape the future of global industry.
