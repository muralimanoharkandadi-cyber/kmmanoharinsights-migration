Scientists in South Korea have engineered a van der Waals crystal that mimics how biological neurons learn and remember. 1. THE PATTERN INTERRUPT

For decades, computing has followed a simple rule: process information, store information, and move data back and forth between separate components.

The human brain works differently.

It processes, learns, adapts, and stores memories simultaneously.

Now scientists have engineered a crystal that behaves surprisingly like a living neural network.

If this technology scales, it could dramatically reduce AI energy consumption while enabling machines that learn more naturally.

The real question is:

Are we witnessing the beginning of truly brain-inspired hardware?

2. WHAT JUST HAPPENED?

A team led by Professor Taesung Kim at Sungkyunkwan University has developed an optoelectronic synaptic device capable of learning through light signals.

Unlike conventional semiconductor devices, this system uses an engineered van der Waals crystal structure that closely resembles biological neuronal architecture.

The innovation emerged from a single-step plasma sulfurization process that transformed part of a rhenium selenide crystal into a nanocrystalline layer while preserving the underlying crystal structure.

This dual-layer architecture behaves similarly to biological ion channels and intracellular neural environments.

The device demonstrated:

  • Long-term memory
  • Short-term memory
  • Learning and forgetting cycles
  • Synaptic plasticity
  • High image-recognition performance

In simple terms:

Scientists have created a crystal that learns using light in a way that resembles how neurons learn.

3. THE SIMPLE EXPLANATION

Imagine a city where every traffic signal constantly changes according to traffic flow.

That is similar to how neurons operate.

Human brains continuously adjust the strength of connections between neurons based on experience.

Scientists call this synaptic plasticity.

Traditional computers do not naturally possess this capability.

Instead, they separate memory and computation.

The new crystal-based device combines both functions.

Think of it like:

A road network that simultaneously carries vehicles, analyzes traffic, learns patterns, and stores historical information.

When light strikes the crystal, sulfur ions move through nanoscale pathways.

This movement changes electrical conductivity, effectively creating a memory of previous signals.

The stronger the signal history, the stronger the learned response.

That behavior closely resembles biological learning.

4. THE CORE MECHANISM

The heart of the breakthrough lies in a specially engineered van der Waals crystal made from rhenium selenide (ReSe₂).

Scientists subjected the material to an argon and hydrogen sulfide plasma treatment.

This transformed the crystal into two distinct layers:

Upper Layer

Nanocrystalline ReSe₂

Lower Layer

Single-crystalline ReSe₂

The upper layer contains numerous grain boundaries.

These boundaries act like biological ion gates.

Sulfur ions move through these pathways when they are exposed to optical stimulation.

The lower layer acts as a stable environment where information can be retained.

Here's the twist:

Instead of fighting ionic movement—a major problem in conventional devices—the researchers transformed it into a controlled learning mechanism.

That single insight changed everything.

5. THE BREAKTHROUGH SHIFT

Previous neuromorphic devices suffered from several limitations:

  • Random ionic migration
  • Interface instability
  • Material defects
  • Poor scalability
  • Low reproducibility

Researchers often struggled to control memory behavior consistently.

The SKKU team approached the problem differently.

Instead of adding multiple layers and complex structures, they redesigned the crystal itself.

Using one plasma treatment, they created a naturally integrated learning architecture.

The result:

A device that behaves predictably while retaining the advantages of layered van der Waals materials.

This elegant materials-engineering solution may become more important than the device itself.
